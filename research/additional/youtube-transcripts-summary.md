## Expert: Dan Petrovic

### VIDEO 01:

Title: AI Search Mechanics | SEO Unplugged #37

Link: https://youtu.be/jIKBAcGMNBs?si=ywErfEIw-Q-vhbn4

Date: ~6 months before (≈Dec 2025)

Summary: A podcast interview where Dan explains what "AI search" actually is mechanically: a presentation layer sitting on top of traditional organic search results, where a model receives the SERP and decides what to surface rather than showing raw links. He stresses that AI search and traditional search share more than 90% of the same underlying pipeline, with the main addition being a thin layer of agentic filtering and decision-making on top. He pushes back on treating AI as a black box, arguing practitioners now have more visibility into ranking systems than ever, since you can directly ask models like Gemini why they selected a given source.

Key Insights:
- AI search is best understood as a presentation layer over traditional search, not a separate retrieval system
- Over 90% of the technical pipeline (crawling, rendering, indexing, ranking) is identical to classic SEO
- You can now directly query models (e.g., Gemini) about why they selected or excluded a source, an unprecedented level of transparency
- Search has "gone AI" and AI has "gone search" simultaneously; the two are converging, not diverging
- Treating AI as mysterious rather than studying its mechanics is a practitioner risk, not a reality of the technology


### VIDEO 02:

Title: AI SEO Deep Dive

Link: https://youtu.be/-f9QCqTqdTA?si=0ZgjmbU-0Q8RuGdZ

- The fundamentals of what a brand must do (publish content, optimize for intent) haven't changed even though the surface (AI Mode, ChatGPT) looks radically different


## Expert: Bernard Huang

### VIDEO 01:

Title: How to Get Your Brand Cited by AI

Link: https://youtu.be/mojvkc9CTWc?si=0svwJkIIBu7NMSn-

Date: ~3 months before (≈March 2026)

Summary: A BuzzStream podcast interview where Bernard breaks down the three-layer mechanism by which AI generates answers: a training data layer, a grounding layer (where the model performs live searches to validate and freshen its answer), and a personalization layer. He argues the fastest lever for influencing AI citations isn't the training data layer (slow, expensive) but the grounding layer, by creating content that targets how AI researches a topic in real time. He also recommends brands identify their core commercial prompts and run digital PR campaigns targeting publications that already rank page one on Google for that query, since high-ranking sites get both faster crawling and heavier model weighting.

Key Insights:
- AI answers are built from three layers: training data, real-time grounding, and personalization; most influence happens at the grounding layer
- The fastest way to influence AI citations is targeting what a model searches for while answering, not trying to alter training data
- Digital PR campaigns should target publications that already rank page one on Google for your target commercial query
- Links matter less than being mentioned on a site Google and AI models already trust and weight heavily
- Traffic is no longer the right AEO success metric; track brand mention frequency and page citation frequency instead

## Expert: Britney Muller

### VIDEO 01:

Title: LLMs Don't Rank Anything. So What Are You Optimizing For?

Link: https://youtu.be/mJ8ipSF97AA?si=dDUFEvQuNhQam_Lu

Date: ~1 month before (≈May 2026)

Summary: A SearchPilot webinar (hosted by Will Critchlow) where Britney lays out her core contrarian thesis: LLMs do not rank anything. They probabilistically predict the next word, with no qualitative assessment of sources and no caching of which URL specific training content came from. She argues this is the single biggest misconception practitioners carry over from traditional SEO thinking, and that "domain authority" as a concept doesn't transfer cleanly to how LLMs actually process brand mentions during training.

Key Insights:
- LLMs are probabilistic next-word predictors, not ranking or retrieval systems; there is no equivalent of "domain authority" inside the base model
- Models don't cache or track which specific URL training content originated from; they only retain patterns of brand mention frequency
- Running the identical prompt repeatedly will produce different outputs; variability is fundamental to the technology, not a bug to optimize around
- Practitioners conflate LLM "mentions" with traditional search "rankings," which leads to flawed optimization strategies
- The disconnect between giving an answer and providing a path to a source (a URL) is framed as one of the LLM's biggest current limitations

### VIDEO 02:

Title: Master Semantic SEO and AI Agents

He explains the importance of timing content readiness around Google's core update cycles, describes splitting topical maps into "core" (monetization-focused) and "outer" (historical-data-building) sections, and introduces "source context": the idea that search engines cluster competing sites by type (affiliate, aggregator, bank, etc.) before ranking within each cluster, meaning content strategy must account for what category of source already dominates a given query.

- Sites should be made "ready" roughly 20 days before an anticipated core update to maximize the chance of being tested and adopted into rankings
- Topical maps should split into a "core" section (direct monetization) and an "outer" section (location/context-agnostic content that builds trust and feeds authority back to the core)
- Query augmentation (what the industry calls "query fan-out") is described as Google reformulating one query roughly 100 different ways internally before constructing an answer
